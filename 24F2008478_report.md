## 1. Introduction

### What is the problem?
The core challenge addressed in this project is the automated diagnostic classification of thoracic pathologies using Chest X-ray (CXR) images. Specifically, it involves a multi-class classification task over 20 distinct labels. The model must analyze monochrome radiographic data to identify one of 19 specific disease states—ranging from localized abnormalities like Nodules and Masses to systemic conditions like Pneumonia and Edema—or categorize the image as "No Finding". The output requirement is a strictly one-hot encoded vector, meaning the model must decide on the single most probable clinical finding per patient.

### Why is it important?
Radiology departments worldwide face an overwhelming volume of chest radiographs, which are the primary tool for diagnosing cardiopulmonary diseases. However, the interpretation process is:

- **Subjective:** Different radiologists may interpret subtle opacities differently.
- **Error-Prone:** High-volume environments lead to diagnostic fatigue, resulting in "missed" findings.
- **High Stakes:** The competition logic imposes a -5 penalty for False Negatives (FN). In medical terms, a False Negative (Type II error) is far more catastrophic than a False Positive (Type I error). Missing a life-threatening condition like Pneumothorax or Effusion can lead to immediate patient mortality, whereas a false alarm only leads to further non-invasive testing. Therefore, creating a sensitivity-optimized model is paramount for clinical safety.

### What is the dataset?
The dataset (26-t-1-dl-gen-ainppe-1) comprises high-resolution chest radiographs. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) revealed:

- **High Resolution Variance:** Images varied in initial dimensions and contrast quality.
- **Target Distribution:** 20 classes representing common and rare pathologies.
- **Class Imbalance:** A "long-tail" distribution where "No Finding" and "Infiltration" are common, while conditions like "Hernia" are extremely rare, creating a challenge for standard objective functions.
The dataset contains 51,043 training images and 17,015 test images. It features a high degree of class imbalance, where "No Finding" is common, but conditions like "Hernia" or "Fibrosis" are rare. Labels are provided in a one-hot encoded format.

### What is your objective?
My primary objective was to architect an end-to-end Deep Learning pipeline that:

1. Enhances low-contrast medical features using Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE).
2. Leverages a feature-dense architecture (DenseNet121) to capture subtle abnormalities.
3. Optimizes a custom Asymmetric Loss function to minimize the heavy -5 FN penalty.
4. Provides a deployment-ready submission adhering to the one-hot encoding constraint.

## 2. Methodology

### Models Used: DenseNet121
I selected **DenseNet121** (Densely Connected Convolutional Network) as the primary architecture after comparing it against ResNet50 and EfficientNet.

- **Why DenseNet121?** In traditional CNNs, as information passes through many layers, it can vanish or become "diluted." DenseNet connects each layer to every other layer in a feed-forward fashion.
- **Feature Preservation:** For thoracic pathologies, the difference between a healthy lung and "Infiltration" might be a few faint pixels. DenseNet’s "Dense Blocks" ensure that low-level structural information from the early layers is preserved and concatenated with high-level semantic features in the deeper layers. This "collective knowledge" is essential for identifying subtle medical patterns.

### Preprocessing Steps
1.  **CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization):** Standard X-rays often have overlapping intensities. I converted images to **LAB color space** and applied CLAHE to the 'L' (Lightness) channel with a `clipLimit=2.0`. This enhances local contrast without amplifying background noise, making vascular markings and lung parenchyma significantly clearer.
2.  **Resizing & Interpolation:** Images were resized to 224x224 using bilinear interpolation. This resolution was chosen as it aligns with the receptive field of the pre-trained DenseNet weights while maintaining a manageable computational footprint.
3.  **Normalization:** Applied Z-score normalization using ImageNet statistics (mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]). This stabilizes the gradient descent process during the initial fine-tuning phase.

### Advanced Techniques
- **Transfer Learning:** I utilized weights pre-trained on the IMAGENET1K_V1 dataset. The final classification head was replaced with a GlobalAveragePooling layer followed by a Linear layer with 20 outputs to map features to the specific pathology classes.
- **Asymmetric Weighted Loss:** I developed a `CombinedAsymmetricLoss` with a **weight of 5.0** for disease classes and **1.0** for 'No Finding'. This forces the model to prioritize disease detection to avoid the competition's specific FN penalty logic.
- **Data Augmentation:** Implemented `RandomHorizontalFlip` to simulate anatomical positioning variations across the patient population.

## 3. Experiments

### What experiments you performed
I conducted an incremental series of experiments to isolate the impact of architecture and logic:
1.  **Architecture Search:** Compared a Custom CNN vs. ResNet50 vs. DenseNet121.
2.  **Loss Function Tuning:** Investigated how the -5 penalty could be addressed by comparing standard Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) against my custom Weighted Asymmetric Loss.
3.  **Preprocessing Impact:** Introduced CLAHE enhancement while keeping the architecture constant. This led to a noticeable improvement in identifying "Infiltration" and "Atelectasis."

### What variations you tried
- **Learning Rate (LR):** I initially tried **1e-3**, which led to unstable validation loss. I reduced this to **1e-4**, which provided stable convergence and allowed the model to fine-tune effectively.
- **Batch Size:** I compared **16** and **32**. Batch size 32 was selected to provide stable gradient estimates and utilize the Kaggle GPU efficiently.
- **Scheduler:** I implemented `ReduceLROnPlateau` with a factor of 0.1 and patience of 2. This allowed the model to decrease the learning rate when validation loss stagnated, ensuring optimal convergence.

### Validation Strategy
I utilized an **85/15 train-validation split** using `train_test_split` with a fixed random seed (42). 

- **Monitoring:** Tracked `val_loss` and `val_accuracy` per epoch.
- **Overfitting Guard:** Monitored the gap between training and validation loss. My final run (Train Loss: 0.0053, Val Loss: 0.0062) indicated the model generalized well without memorizing the training noise.
