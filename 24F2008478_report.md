## 1. Introduction

### What is the problem?
The core objective of this project is to develop an automated deep learning pipeline for the multi-class classification of 20 distinct thoracic pathologies from Chest X-ray (CXR) images. The task requires a mutually exclusive prediction for each image, categorized into 19 active disease states (e.g., Pneumonia, Cardiomegaly, Effusion) or a "No Finding" baseline. The final output must be a one-hot encoded vector where exactly one class is predicted per patient.

### Why is it important?
In clinical settings, radiographs are the most frequent imaging examinations, yet their interpretation is highly subjective and prone to human error, especially in high-volume environments. Automated detection acts as a vital decision-support tool. Furthermore, the competition logic imposes a **-5 penalty for False Negatives (FN)**. This reflects a critical medical priority: missing a pathology (Type II error) is significantly more dangerous than a false alarm. Developing an AI that minimizes missed diagnoses while maintaining accuracy is essential for patient safety.

### What is the dataset?
The dataset (26-t-1-dl-gen-ainppe-1) comprises high-resolution chest radiographs. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) revealed:
- **High Resolution Variance:** Images varied in initial dimensions and contrast quality.
- **Target Distribution:** 20 classes representing common and rare pathologies.
- **Class Imbalance:** A "long-tail" distribution where "No Finding" and "Infiltration" are common, while conditions like "Hernia" are extremely rare, creating a challenge for standard objective functions.

### What is your objective?
My objective was to design a robust pipeline that:
1. Enhances low-contrast medical features using Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE).
2. Leverages a feature-dense architecture (DenseNet121) to capture subtle abnormalities.
3. Optimizes a custom Asymmetric Loss function to minimize the heavy -5 FN penalty.

## 2. Methodology

### Models Used: DenseNet121
I selected **DenseNet121** as the primary architecture after comparing it against ResNet50 and EfficientNet.
- **Why DenseNet121?** Unlike standard CNNs, DenseNet connects every layer to every other layer. In medical imaging, subtle pathological indicators (like small nodules or faint pleural effusion) can be lost in deeper layers of traditional networks. DenseNet’s "dense connectivity" ensures maximum feature reuse and maintains the original signal integrity throughout the network, making it superior for capturing fine-grained thoracic patterns.

### Preprocessing Steps
1.  **CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization):** Standard X-rays often have overlapping intensities. I converted images to **LAB color space** and applied CLAHE to the 'L' (Lightness) channel with a `clipLimit=2.0`. This enhances local contrast without amplifying background noise, making vascular markings and lung parenchyma significantly clearer.
2.  **Resizing:** Standardized all images to **224x224 pixels** to balance diagnostic detail preservation with computational efficiency.
3.  **Normalization:** Applied ImageNet-specific mean `[0.485, 0.456, 0.406]` and standard deviation `[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]`.

### Advanced Techniques
- **Transfer Learning:** Utilized pre-trained **IMAGENET1K_V1** weights to leverage sophisticated visual feature extraction, followed by fine-tuning on medical data.
- **Asymmetric Weighted Loss:** I developed a `CombinedAsymmetricLoss` with a **weight of 5.0** for disease classes and **1.0** for 'No Finding'. This forces the model to prioritize disease detection to avoid the competition's specific FN penalty logic.
- **Data Augmentation:** Implemented `RandomHorizontalFlip` to simulate anatomical positioning variations across the patient population.

## 3. Experiments

### What experiments you performed
I conducted an incremental series of experiments to isolate the impact of architecture and logic:
1.  **Architecture Search:** Compared a Custom CNN vs. ResNet50 vs. DenseNet121.
2.  **Loss Function Tuning:** Investigated how the -5 penalty could be addressed by comparing standard Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) against my custom Weighted Asymmetric Loss.
3.  **Preprocessing Impact:** Tested the model with and without CLAHE contrast enhancement.

### What variations you tried
- **Learning Rate (LR):** I initially tried **1e-3**, which led to unstable validation loss. I reduced this to **1e-4**, which provided stable convergence and allowed the model to fine-tune effectively.
- **Batch Size:** I compared **16** and **32**. Batch size 32 was selected to provide stable gradient estimates and utilize the Kaggle GPU efficiently.
- **Scheduler:** I implemented `ReduceLROnPlateau` with a factor of 0.1 and patience of 2. This allowed the model to decrease the learning rate when validation loss stagnated, ensuring optimal convergence.

### Validation Strategy
I utilized an **85/15 train-validation split** using `train_test_split` with a fixed random seed (42). 
- **Monitoring:** Tracked `val_loss` and `val_accuracy` per epoch.
- **Overfitting Guard:** Monitored the gap between training and validation loss. My final run (Train Loss: 0.0053, Val Loss: 0.0062) indicated the model generalized well without memorizing the training noise.

## 5. Conclusion

### Summary of Approach
My approach successfully integrated **DenseNet121** with a custom **Combined Asymmetric Loss** to explicitly address the competition's high penalty for missed diseases. By utilizing **CLAHE** to enhance radiographs and **Transfer Learning** for rapid convergence, I created a pipeline that satisfies both the technical accuracy requirements and the formatting constraints (one-hot encoding via argmax).

### Key Learnings
1.  **Medical-Specific Preprocessing:** Contrast enhancement (CLAHE) is essential for X-rays as standard resizing can obscure critical pathological details.
2.  **Loss over Architecture:** Modifying the loss function to match specific scoring penalties (like the -5 for FN) is more effective than simply increasing the model depth.
3.  **Class Bias:** Addressing class imbalance through pos_weights is vital in medical datasets where the "Normal" class dominates.
